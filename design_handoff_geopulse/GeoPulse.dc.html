Methodology

How GeoPulse scores the planet

GeoPulse treats every country like a fleet of services. Each domain — Economy, Markets, Relations, Conflict — emits raw signals that are standardised against the country's own recent history, mapped to a discrete state, and then combined into a single composite. The goal is a calm, comparable read on planetary health, not a news feed.

1 · Z-scores against a 90-day baseline

Every metric is scored relative to itself, not to other countries. We compute a rolling mean μ and standard deviation σ over a trailing 90-day window and express today's value as a z-score, z = (x − μ) / σ. A z-score isolates unusual-for-this-country behaviour, so Argentina's structurally high inflation only trips a state when it accelerates beyond its own norm.

State thresholds on the z-axis

Bands are symmetric: a metric is unhealthy whether it moves sharply up or down relative to baseline. Directionality (good vs bad) is applied per-metric before mapping.

2 · Worst-of composite

A country's composite state is the worst of its domain states — never an average. One disrupted domain disrupts the country. This mirrors an operations dashboard: a single failing service degrades the whole page, and averaging would mask real trouble.

3 · Hysteresis

States are sticky. A metric must cross a threshold and hold for a confirmation window before the state changes — enter at |z| = 1.0, but only recover below |z| = 0.7. This dead-band prevents a jittery metric from flickering between states and keeps the map calm.

4 · Staleness is a state, not a gap

If a metric's freshest observation is older than its expected cadence (e.g. quarterly GDP more than ~100 days old), the metric — and any composite that depends on it — is marked stale, rendered in grey with a diagonal hatch. Stale never masquerades as healthy: an absent signal is reported as absent, with its source and age shown on every value.
